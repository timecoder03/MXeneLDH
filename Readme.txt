Layered double hydroxides (LDHs) are attractive for electrochemical sensing because of their electroactive and hydroxyl-rich surfaces. However, their practical sensing performance is often limited by poor electrical conductivity. In contrast, Ti3C2Tx MXenes exhibit high electrical conductivity and hydrophilic surface chemistry, but they can lack sufficient analyte-specific adsorption sites. Here, we develop a Ni0.5Fe0.25Mn0.25 layered double hydroxide/Ti3C2Tx MXene hybrid composite (NFM-MX) for dopamine sensing, where Ti3C2Tx serves as a conductive scaffold, and NFM-LDH provides hydroxyl-rich molecular recognition sites. Density functional theory calculations reveal that dopamine adsorbs moderately on mixed-terminated Ti3C2Tx, with adsorption energies around -0.6 eV. In contrast, NFM-LDH enables significantly stronger dopamine adsorption, with the most stable configuration reaching -1.5 eV. The enhanced adsorption on NFM-LDH proceeds through an acid-base-type adsorption process, where catechol deprotonation forms a catecholate-like species stabilized by multiple cooperative hydrogen-bonding interactions with the hydroxylated LDH surface. Furthermore, the charge redistribution at the NFM-MX interface leads to the formation of an interfacial dipole and electronic coupling across the van der Waals contact, while preserving the intrinsic properties of both components. Electrochemical measurements demonstrate improved dopamine sensing performance for the NFM-MX electrode, achieving a detection limit of 0.109 µM under optimum conditions. Competitive discrimination against common interference molecules was observed.
